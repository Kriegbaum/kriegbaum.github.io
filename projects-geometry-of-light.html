Geometry of Light

Luftwerk

Temporary Installation

Concept:

This installation took place in the Barcelona Pavillion designed by Mies van der Rohe. Contracting laser levels were used to highlight aspects of the architecture, and moved in sequences

My Role

Luftwerk selected and sourced the lasers. From there, I was responsible for figuring out how to control them and building a system to execute the control patterns. The installation needed to be mostly wireless, as there are not many places to conceal cable in the pavillion. Once onsite, I also needed to create documentation for the installation process to give to the pavillion staff, as the installation was partially struck and restored on a daily basis.

Technical Design

The project had a centralized brain located in the basement with a trigger button that allowed the pavillion staff to easily start up the installation at its opening hours. The brain propagated control signals out to the lasers via a small network setup that was placed in the one closed off area in the pavillion.

With the inclusion of music, I placed the composer's playback computer on my network, and sent a sync pulse every 5 minutes via OSC, communicating to Qlab, that kept the light show and the music in step with one another.

Challenges

There were many layers of technical challenges in this project. The first barrier to controlling the lasers was that the only available control platform was a smartphone app that operated over bluetooth. By reverse engineering the bluetooth protocol for the laser, I was able to take control of the device from an arbitrary bluetooth soruce.
From there, the limitations of bluetooth as a control platform was my primary problem. There were 13 bluetooth devices in the field needing control. Most bluetooth devices do not support this many concurrent connections, but more importantly, the broadcast range and reliablility were not good enough for a site this size with heavy foot traffic. In order to ensure stability in the control system, I created a network of transponders with raspberry pi zeroes. The control signals completed most of their journey through space via a long range wifi access point to the transponders, and then made the final leg of the journey over bluetooth. Transponders were never placed more than 5 feet from their receiving bluetooth units.

The preceeding design choices made this project into a specialized system, which I was not able to be on site to support once it opened. For that reason, I created a bridge between the wifi broadcasting out of the pavillion's bookstore and my isolated network that allowed me remote access to the site in case support was required once I had left.
